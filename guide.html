Part 1: Service Overview

Service Structure

There are three transplant surgery fellows. Each month, one is assigned to the kidney service, one to the liver service, and one is the donor fellow (covering organ procurements, living donor cases, HPB cases, and general surgery cases on transplant patients).

Each fellow covers their own service overnight Monday–Thursday. A single on-call fellow covers all patients from Friday 5pm through Monday 6am.

Shared patient lists in Epic correspond to the three services and should be accessible to all team members. Contact a fellow if you don’t have access.

Weekly Schedule

A weekly email goes out every Sunday with subservice assignments (kidney / liver / donor) and OR cases for the week. Residents are responsible for assigning a resident to every case listed.

Pertinent handoff and sign-out information is maintained in the “Transplant” handoff section in Epic, managed by the fellows.

Rounds

  • Morning rounds are subservice-specific (kidney vs. liver). Check with your fellow about timing — typically starts around 0700 ± 30 minutes depending on census.
  • Multidisciplinary Zoom rounds (with hepatology/nephrology, pharmacy, APPs, social work) are held Monday–Friday:
    • Kidney: 0800: https://jhjhm.zoom.us/j/91503454356
    • Liver: 0830: https://jhjhm.zoom.us/j/92373551229
  • Tuesday 0730: Dr. Cameron’s teaching rounds for residents/medical students (meet at his office in the Ross Building). Residents and med students are excused from morning work rounds at this time. The night intern is not expected to attend.
  • Other conferences:
    • Dept. of Surgery M&M — Tuesdays 0630 (link in weekly email)
    • Living donor liver selection meeting — Wednesdays 1500
    • Dept. of Surgery Grand Rounds — Thursdays 0730 (link in weekly email)
    • Liver candidate listing meeting — Thursdays 1400

Communication

  • Texts are fine for overnight updates or brief questions (individual fellows may have preferences). Call if no response within 15–20 minutes, in an emergency, or if a detailed conversation is needed.
  • All new admissions must be communicated to the fellow promptly — including expected admissions.
  • The person picking up the pager each morning must be signed in to first call for all primary patients.
  • Start a group text with all fellows and residents at the beginning of the block. The night before, confirm who is picking up the pager and who is covering which case.

Part 2: Resident Responsibilities

Night Resident

  • Sign in as first call for all patients (except 9E and CVSICU)
  • If a fellow is in the OR overnight, come down to the OR to give updates in person whenever possible
  • Texts are fine for overnight updates or brief questions (individual fellows may have preferences). Call if no response within 15–20 minutes, in an emergency, or if a detailed conversation is needed.
  • All new admissions must be communicated to the fellow promptly — including expected admissions.
  • Ensure labs are drawn for patients admitted for kidney transplant; consult nephrology and help facilitate pre-op dialysis if needed
  • Obtain procedure and blood consent for kidney transplant recipients; fellows will do procedure consent for liver transplant recipients
  • In the mornings, send a summary text to all fellows, detailing overnight events template below

LIVER

GRAY: Emesis overnight, NG placed

MEEK: No updates

PRES: aPTT subtherapeutic, infusion increased by 1U

KIDNEY

MASS: No updates.

MACK: Oliguric, unresponsive to fluid bolus, foley patent, duplex pending

DOYLE: Hyperkalemic, Lasix 80 IV, WBK pending

DONOR

KYLE: No updates

Daily Responsibilities

  • Pager pickup: Every morning, one resident picks up the pager from the night intern by 0500 Monday–Friday. Rotate equitably; account for education days. The pager holder should not have education responsibilities that day. Hand off to APPs when they arrive (~0800, or ~0700 on Saturdays) if you’ll be in the OR.
  • Consult coverage: One resident must remain available until 5pm on weekdays to see consults. If nothing is pending, designate that person, notify all fellows, then others may leave. If the designated person is in the OR, reassign before leaving — be equitable and transparent.
  • Active rounding: Listen on rounds and when the list is run with APPs. Identify what tasks can be done for individual patients and help execute them. If you have no tasks, read, study, or reassess patients who need ongoing evaluation.
  • Patient transport: You may be asked to assist with transport to CT, IR, etc. Be available.

Case Coverage

Transplant operations are excellent opportunities to learn anatomy and operative technique. Residents should be assigned to every case — including evenings and weekends.

When assigned to a case, you are responsible for:

  • Completing the H&P (with thorough medication review — do not rely solely on auto-populated Epic data)
  • Interval H&P addendum on the day of surgery if H&P was done earlier
  • Site marking (where applicable)
  • Blood consent
  • Surgical consent (fellows consent liver transplant recipients, living donors [kidney and liver], and pediatric patients; residents consent all others — see consent section below)
  • Ensuring intraop medications are ordered (methylpred for all transplants, thymo/simulect for kidney/kp recipients)
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis — anesthesia has ancef/flagyl/clinda in Pyxis; other antibiotics must be ordered

Residents must assign a resident to all cases in the weekly email, split equitably. The kidney resident should prioritize rounds when possible. Donor runs should rotate so everyone gets exposure. If you have an intern on service, include them equitably.

Weekend Responsibilities

The weekend rounding resident is responsible for:

  • Rounding Saturday and Sunday mornings with the on-call fellow
  • Assisting APPs with patient care, notes, and orders
  • Picking up the pager from the overnight Friday and Saturday night residents
  • Being available to back up simultaneous transplants (e.g., covering a kidney transplant while the on-call fellow is in a liver transplant)

If you want to leave during weekend downtime, this must be cleared by the fellow. You must be able to return promptly for consults (Saturday: morning–midafternoon; Sunday: morning–5pm). You may occasionally need to return in the evening for simultaneous transplant coverage.

Donor Call

If assigned to donor call, you are not expected to round. You must remain available and be prepared to arrive within 30 minutes. There is usually a few hours’ notice for procurements, but not always. Answer your phone — if you can’t be reached, coverage will be reassigned and you may miss the case.

Education

Cameron teaching rounds are Tuesdays 7:30–8:30 AM. You are excused from the OR and morning rounds at this time. Complete all assigned tasks before leaving for education.

Track your own duty hours. If you anticipate a duty-hour issue, notify your fellow well in advance — adjustments are typically made by adding time off during the week, not by changing the call or weekend schedule.

Documentation

  • Daily progress notes on inpatients are written by the APPs — not a resident responsibility.
  • Residents write H&Ps on new admissions and consult patients.
  • Residents complete preop H&Ps (or interval H&P addendums) on all scheduled cases.

Part 3: Clinical Protocols

Consults

  • Pre-transplant patients (even if listed) are typically admitted to a medical service.
  • Cirrhotic patients with a surgical problem (appendicitis, hernia, etc.) are occasionally managed by the Halsted service, unless the patient is listed at JH – Discuss with Liver Fellow
  • PD catheter placements and removals – Discuss with the Donor fellow.
  • HPB/pancreatic cancer consults: If a patient has been seen previously by an HPB surgeon at Hopkins, that surgeon has first right of refusal. Discuss all HPB consults with the donor fellow.

Consents

  • All patients require a blood consent preoperatively and often at admission/readmission.
  • Fellows consent liver transplant recipients, living donors (kidney and liver), and pediatric patients. Residents are responsible for all other surgical consents.
  • For surgical consents, include the primary attending’s name as the surgeon, and use SmartPhrase .txpaddprov to add the remainder of the attendings/fellows in the free text section

Kidney Transplant

Admission Checklist

COVID test: Order only if patient is symptomatic. If needed: SARS-CoV-2 NAT, NP Swab, STAT. Call lab (5-6510, option 1) if not resulted within an hour.

  • Place preop orders: “JHH Adult Surgery Renal Transplant Pre-Op Admission” (must be in Admissions tab). For DVT risk, select “high risk of bleeding” as contraindication for pharmacological DVT ppx — do not order DVT ppx preoperatively.
  • Medication reconciliation: Review directly with patient. Do not rely on auto-populated Epic data, especially for BP meds.
  • Immunogenetics crossmatch: T and B cell crossmatch + Flow Cytometric. Select “kidney,” “recipient.” Patient on thymoglobulin in last month? Typically no.
  • STAT labs: BMP, CBC, whole blood K and Na, type and screen — send as soon as possible.
  • Infectious risk labs: HBV, HCV, HIV (in order set — send before OR, results not needed).
  • EBV/CMV repeat labs if indicated: Send before OR, results not needed, but must check the box.
  • NPO with sips with meds. IVF not required unless preemptive and expected to wait a long time.
  • Follow up on potassium. Alert fellow if K > 5.0 (may need HD preop). If K 5.0–5.5, confirm with anesthesia faculty (get names). If HD needed, call renal fellow immediately — often run a short 1–2h session. Confirm with OR nursing and anesthesia before going from HD to OR; bring the chart.
  • Consents: blood consent + kidney transplant surgical consent.
  • Intraop meds: Order set → “Abdominal Transplant Intra-Op Meds Focused” → methylpred 500 mg + 1.5 mg/kg thymo (round up to nearest 25 mg; max ~150 mg). Include peripheral version with heparin unless heparin allergy. Sign and hold.

H&P Requirements

Your H&P must include all of the following — you may be asked to revise if any element is missing: (SmartPhrase: .txphnp)

  • Cause of renal failure (biopsy-proven? prior treatment? prior steroids?)
  • Dialysis type (HD vs. PD), schedule, access (line/fistula/graft), date and completeness of last session, prior access history or complications
  • Residual urine output — quantify
  • Comorbidities: HTN, DM, and current medication regimen (verify with patient directly)
  • Cardiac history or new symptoms; recent echo/stress test; recent cross-sectional imaging — note and alert fellow
  • Allergies (verify)
  • Surgical history — all abdominal scars, PD catheter location, prior abdominal procedures
  • Femoral and distal pulses bilaterally — palpate and document
  • Contact information — verify post-op contact; confirm accurate number is in Epic

Consenting a Kidney Transplant Recipient

Procedure: Deceased Donor Kidney Transplant RIGHT (or LEFT), possible right/left/intra-abdominal placement

Add as applicable:

  • Possible PD catheter removal (for living donor recipients if on PD)
  • Possible HD catheter placement (if no HD access — PD patients and preemptive transplants)
  • Possible RIGHT/LEFT nephrectomy (if polycystic kidneys require space)

Key categories to cover:

  1. Postoperative expectations: 10–20 cm curved incision, 1–2 drains (go home with patient), admit to 9W, ~5 day LOS, Foley for 3–5 days minimum, arterial line day 1, early ambulation POD0, diet progression (sips/chips POD0, CLD POD1), no heavy lifting 4–6 weeks
  2. General surgical risks: Bleeding (~25% need transfusion), pain, wound complications, infection, injury to adjacent structures (bowel, vessels)
  3. Transplant-specific risks: Vascular — artery/vein strictures (<3%), bleeding/hematoma (most common early reoperation). Urologic — ureteral leak/stricture (20–30% risk; most managed with stent, few need reoperation). DGF — rate increasing to ~40% of DDKT; patient may leave on dialysis; rarely, graft never functions
  4. Higher-risk kidneys: HCV (diagnosed POD5, treated 8–12 weeks), HBV (lifelong entecavir/letermovir), high KDPI >85% (increased long-term failure risk, may take longer to function)
  5. Medical risks: Moderate cardiac/thromboembolic risk given comorbidities
  6. Immunosuppression: Infection risk (antifungal, antiviral, antibiotic ppx for 1 month to 1 year); cancer risk (skin cancer, PTLD/lymphoma — treatable with IS modification)
  • Verify and document if donor is HBV+, HCV+, HIV+, EBV+ to EBV− recipient, KDPI >85%, COVID status. PHS increased infectious risk is no longer standard language, but risk behaviors in the last month must be discussed and documented. This information will be in an Epic group chat message from the coordinator; if unavailable, check with the fellow

Intraop Setup

  • Ask nursing to call pharmacy for methylpred and thymo when patient arrives to the operating room (takes time to compound)
  • Anesthesia: intubate, A-line, IV placement
  • Foley with methylene blue (1 cc in 1 L); Foley over the right leg, taped in place; clean ports with EtOH swab, attach bladder irrigation tubing; test line; ensure roller stop accessible to anesthesia
  • Arms out; Bookwalter post on contralateral side (Philosophe uses ipsilateral)
  • Ancef for abx ppx unless contraindicated (anesthesia has in Pyxis — no order needed)
  • Some attendings give intraop heparin prior to anastomosis for preemptive transplants (no order needed)

Immediate Postop Care

Clarify with attending/fellow before leaving the OR:

  • Foley duration (typically 3 days; 4-5 days for small/thin bladder or ureteral reconstruction concerns)
  • Thymo goal (mg/kg and total dose)
  • Sensitization (PRA) and any DSA
  • Fluid plan: Default — 30 cc/hr + cc-for-cc UOP replacement × 24h (max 1000 cc/hr for most attendings). Dr. Philosophe: 30 cc/hr + 0.5 cc per cc UOP.
  • When to start tacrolimus: most attendings start POD#1 at 1 mg BID immediate-release (Prograf)

Order set: “JHH Renal Transplant ERAS Pathway Post-Op Admission”

  • Patient goes to HAT (IMC) bed from PACU — ensure nurse calls for bed near end of case
  • Nursing assessments → IMC
  • Fistula checks / extremity protection if fistula or graft present
  • Drains section: defaults to 1 drain — add additional drain if placed
  • Antifungal: “kidney transplant alone recipient” → defaults to fluconazole 200 mg weekly
  • CMV ppx: acyclovir if D−/R−; Valcyte otherwise — confirm based on donor/recipient status
  • PCP prophylaxis: daily Bactrim unless allergy or anticipated DGF
  • Mycophenolate: select
  • Tacrolimus: verify 1 mg BID ordered; change as needed after discussing with attending
  • Uncheck aspirin, heparin (start POD#1 if drains look okay)
  • Update provider care team and code status (JHH Transplant; full code)
  • Adjust Plasmalyte orders
  • Change ultrasound order to STAT; include number of arteries and veins on transplant kidney
  • Diet: sips and chips; advance to CLD if no nausea/vomiting, ultrasound okay, first 6h CBC not concerning
  • DVT ppx: do NOT order until drains reviewed on POD#1
  • Release ultrasound STAT and lab STAT orders (BMP, CBC) immediately; call ultrasound to come to PACU

Night of surgery:

  • Fever and hypotension can be thymo side effects — alert fellow. Can slow infusion to 12h rate (~42 cc/hr; standard 6h rate is ~84 cc/hr).
  • Alert fellow if no UOP or if UOP drops by ≥50% for two consecutive hours
  • Goal SBP 120–160: PRN labetalol
  • Nicardipine infusion for SBP sustained >160, alert fellow if nicardipine drip needed
  • Half thymo rate if SBP < 120, stop if sustained < 120
  • q6h labs × 24h: if anuric and K > 5.5, likely needs dialysis.

Ongoing Postop Care — Kidney

  • CV: Restart home BP meds. Do NOT restart ACE-i or ARBs.
  • Pulm: Wean O2; consider diuresis/HD if volume overloaded.
  • Renal/FEN: Daily Cr, K, BUN, fluid status; need for dialysis? CLD after 6h if H/H stable; advance to regular diet.
  • Heme: Start ASA 81 mg and heparin SQ (BID for elderly/small patients — discuss with attending) on POD#1 if H/H stable and drains non-bloody.
  • ID: Confirm ppx on board, dosed for CrCl. CMV: acyclovir (D−/R−) or Valcyte (all others). Bactrim and fluconazole (both dose-adjust for CrCl).

Immunosuppression:

  • Daily thymo + methylpred until thymo goal complete (max 150 mg/day)
  • MMF 1000 mg BID (reduce to 500 mg BID or space to QID if diarrhea; reduce to 500 BID once tac level is therapeutic)
  • Tacrolimus: follow daily trough; goal 6–10 (discussed daily) immediately postop. Can discharge below goal if tac is detectable and outpatient labs at JHOC are arranged.
  • PT/OT: Not in order set automatically — consult if patient has mobility difficulty.

Liver Transplant

Admission Checklist

  • COVID test: symptomatic only (same as kidney)
  • Place orders: “JHH Adult Surgery Liver Transplant Pre-Op Admission”
    • STAT type and screen
    • Standard pRBCs/FFP unless otherwise directed
    • DVT risk: document “high risk of bleeding”
    • Hepatitis/HIV labs: draw but results not required preop
    • STAT sodium, potassium, CBC, CMP, PT/INR
    • CMV testing as indicated in order set
  • Immunogenetics: Liver alone — “Immunogenetics typings” (2 yellow ACDs) + “Immunogenetics antibody screen” (1 red top). Simultaneous liver-kidney — “Immunogenetics CROSSMATCH” (1 red tube + 4 yellow ACDs).
  • Medication reconciliation: review with patient directly. Do NOT order lactulose if it’s a home med. Hold diuretic, rifaximin, and SBP prophylaxis
  • If patient is on the medicine service: ensure NPO, COVID test sent, and lactulose discontinued. Use the “preop liver for established inpatients” order set. Follow up to confirm labs are actually drawn.
  • Complete H&P noting complications from liver disease and prior abdominal surgeries. Thorough medication reconciliation required. (SmartPhrase: .txphnp)
  • Blood consent (fellow completes surgical consent).

Admissions — General Notes

  • Fellow must always be notified of new admissions.
  • All new admits and postop patients need a first-call provider designated — outside 8am–5pm, use the generic “transplant surgery service pager” or on-call provider.
  • Add new admits to the appropriate shared Epic patient list (kidney/liver/donor).

Handoff format for patients:

Fill in upon admission after seeing the patient, fellow will input additional pertinent information

SmartPhrases - `txpkidneyhandoff`, `txpdonorhandoff`, `txpliverhandoff`

HAL Transfers and Direct Admissions

Patients frequently transfer from outside hospitals or are admitted directly for transplant evaluation or postop complications (hyperkalemia, anemia, elevated LFTs or Cr, etc.).

These patients are often undifferentiated and may arrive sicker than anticipated. See them promptly on arrival. Fellows must always be notified, even if the patient was expected.

Coordinators sometimes admit patients directly overnight or advise them to go to the ED. Watch for Secure Chat messages on these patients — they should be seen promptly even if the ED is involved.

Postop Readmission Routing

  • Postop liver transplant recipients: admitted to transplant service for at least one year. After that, admitted to hepatology (MEG), with exceptions for complicated patients (ongoing biliary issues, PTC/PBDs).
  • Postop kidney/pancreas recipients: admitted to transplant service for the first month postop. After care transfers to outpatient transplant nephrology, readmissions go to the renal transplant service — we are consulted as needed.
  • We do not admit or consult on lung transplant recipients. We are the abdominal transplant surgery service (kidney, liver, pancreas).

Drain, Staple, and Line Removal

  • Central lines: Nursing can remove in patients with normal coagulation. Providers remove in the setting of coagulopathy. Providers also remove tunneled lines/catheters (order lidocaine in Epic; other supplies on 9W).
  • Liver drains: All surgical drains removed prior to discharge. Due to high incidence of postop ascites, drain sites are sutured closed with silk suture (figure-of-8 or U stitch, sometimes multiple). Numb with lidocaine (must order in Epic). Drains numbered 1–3 right to left: #1 behind right lobe, #2 over hilar anastomoses, #3 behind caudate/left IVC.
  • Kidney drains: Patients typically go home with drains. Remove when Foley is out and output is <30 cc/day × 2 consecutive days. No suture closure needed. Label deep drain #1, superficial/subcutaneous drain #2.
  • General surgery/HPB drains: Removed prior to discharge; no suture closure needed unless otherwise directed.
  • Staples: Typically remain 3–4 weeks postoperatively in transplant recipients.
  • Always confirm with the fellow before removing lines, drains and staples.

Procurements

Procurements are led by the donor fellow and are excellent opportunities to see the donor operation and learn anatomy. When the donor fellow is off, on vacation, or in another case, residents may be asked to do procurements with the attending.

Medical students are welcome — check with the fellow about transport capacity and any donor hospital restrictions. Names, birthdates, and weights are required for flight manifests if flying.

Pickup:

Ground floor of Zayed building – Orleans Street entrance (confirm with fellow on the day)

What to bring:

  • Scrubs, government ID (REAL ID), loupes, a jacket (planes are cold)
  • Keep bags minimal — they may need to stay in the OR hallway at the donor hospital
  • The transport van will have snacks/drinks; a meal is typically arranged for the return trip

Timing:

Confirm timing with donor fellow. Donors are often delayed by hours. If you have other responsibilities, be aware there is no guaranteed return time.
